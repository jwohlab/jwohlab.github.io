Our Research

1. Electrochemistry & Photocatalysts

Electrochemistry is the study of chemical reactions (oxidation and reduction) that involve the transfer of electrons, or conversely, the study of electrical energy generated through chemical reactions.

Applications of electrochemistry include electroanalytical chemistry (analyzing biological substances like glucose), development of electrochemical and photocatalysts to improve reaction efficiency, and energy conversion/storage fields such as lithium batteries, fuel cells, and solar cells.

Current Focus:

  • Development of electrochemical sensors for detecting biological substances like dopamine.
  • Study of electrogenerated chemiluminescence (ECL) and sensor development.
  • Research on electrochemical reactions using nanomaterials and catalyst development.

2. Biosensors

A biosensor is a device that qualitatively and quantitatively analyzes biological substances of interest such as DNA, RNA, proteins, neurotransmitters, and viruses.

A representative example is the COVID-19 diagnostic kit. Key to biosensors is designing receptors that selectively bind to target substances, often using immune responses (immunosensors). Detection methods include optical, electrochemical, mass-based, and surface plasmon resonance techniques.

Current Focus:

  • DNA sensors using electrochemical and optical methods.
  • Dopamine sensors (neurotransmitter detection).
  • Influenza virus sensors.

3. Nanochemistry & Nanoplasmonics

Nanochemistry deals with materials in the range of several to hundreds of nanometers. Physical and chemical phenomena at the nanoscale differ significantly from bulk materials, offering unique properties.

Nanoplasmonics involves the interaction of light with free electrons in metal nanostructures, generating surface plasmons. This allows for light manipulation at the nanometer scale, exceeding the diffraction limit of conventional optics.

Current Focus:

  • Synthesis and behavioral study of various nanoparticles and nanostructures.
  • Plasmon-enhanced optical phenomena (fluorescence, scattering, Raman) by controlling nanogaps.
  • Applications in electrochemistry, photocatalysis, and light-emitting materials.
